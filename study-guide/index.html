Investigation Tools

X. The CIE's "Eyes"

We don't just see with our eyes; we see with physics.

1. Moisture Meters (Non-Invasive vs. Invasive)

  • Non-Invasive (Capacitance): Good for fast scanning. Does not damage walls. High chance of "false positives" near metal studs/wires.
  • Invasive (Pin-type): Measures Resistance between pins. This is the Gold Standard for proving actual %MC (Moisture Content).
Thermal Imaging (IR Cameras):

IR cameras detect temperature differentials, not moisture. We look for Evaporative Cooling (cold spots) to find hidden leaks.
*Verify with a moisture meter!*

XI. Specialized Meters

  • Laser Particle Counter: Used to check if our HEPA scrubbers are leaking or to find "spikes" in dust/spores in real-time.
  • PID (Photoionization Detector): Used for VOCs and Formaldehyde. If it has a lamp, it can "see" the chemicals.
  • Anemometer: Measures air velocity (FPM). Essential for calculating CFM and ACH.
CFM = Velocity (FPM) √ó Area (sq. ft)

XII. Boreoscope Inspection

The "Snake Cam." Use this to look for Condition 3 growth inside wall cavities without tearing down the whole wall.

Pro Tip: If you drill a hole for a boreoscope, HEPA vacuum the dust immediately. We don't want to aerosolize the problem we're trying to find!

Safety & Containment

XIII. PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)

We follow the OSHA Respiratory Protection Standard (29 CFR 1910.134).

The Respirator Tier List:

  • N-95: Minimum for small "Condition 2" areas. Disposable.
  • Half-Mask (P100): The standard for most inspections. P100 filters catch oil-based and non-oil-based particles.
  • Full-Face P100: Adds eye protection. Essential if using biocides or in high-spore environments.

XIV. Containment Types (S520)

The goal is Source Isolation.

Level 2: Full Containment

Critical barriers over all openings. Requires a Decon Chamber (Airlock). This is where we MUST maintain our -0.02 in-wc pressure.

Lab Data Analysis

XXI. Reading the "Fungal Signature"

A lab report is just numbers until a CIE interprets the Ecology. We look for imbalances, not just high counts.

Red Flag Indicators:

  • Target Species: Even a "Low" count of Stachybotrys or Chaetomium is a fail inside. They aren't buoyant; if they're in the air, they are growing nearby.
  • Out of Balance: Outdoor is 90% Cladosporium, but Indoor is 90% Penicillium/Aspergillus. This is Fungal Dominance.
  • Hyphal Fragments: High counts of "roots" in the air mean the mold is actively being disturbed or is growing vigorously.

XXII. The "Null Hypothesis"

Assume the indoor air is the same as outdoor. If the data rejects this (higher counts or different species), you have found your source.

CIE Exam Trap: "The indoor count is 400 and outdoor is 4,000. Does it pass?"
Answer: Not necessarily. If that 400 is all Aspergillus and the 4,000 is all Cladosporium, it's a Condition 3 failure.

Building Envelope Defects

Where the "skin" of the building fails.

XXV. The Bulk Water Barrier

If the flashing is wrong, the mold is inevitable. We look for these "Sketched" defects:

1. Kick-out Flashing

Missing at the roof-to-wall intersection. Without it, water runs down the siding and behind the house wrap, rot occurring at the rim joist.

2. The Ice Dam Effect:

Heat escaping from the attic melts snow on the roof. Water runs down to the cold eaves and refreezes, creating a "dam."
Result: Standing water backs up under shingles and into the soffit.

XXVI. Window & Door Fenestrations

  • Head Flashing: Missing "Drip Caps" above windows allow water to track inward.
  • Reverse Laps: When house wrap or flashing is tucked under the layer below it instead of over (like a shingle).

XXVII. The Vapor Barrier "Sandwich"

Never put a vapor barrier on both sides of a wall. This traps moisture inside the wall cavity, leading to "Hidden" Condition 3 growth on the studs.

CIE RULE: Buildings MUST be able to dry in at least one direction (Inside or Outside).
*Look for stained headers!*

Crawlspace & Basement Dynamics

The foundation of the Indoor Environment.

XXVIII. The Stack Effect (Chimney Effect)

Warm air rises and escapes through the attic, creating a negative pressure at the bottom of the house. This literally sucks air up from the crawlspace into the living area.

The 50% Rule:

Up to 50% of the air on the first floor of a home can originate from the crawlspace or basement.
CIE Insight: If there is Condition 3 growth in the crawl, the whole house is likely Condition 2.

XXIX. Capillary Rise (Wicking)

Water can rise through porous masonry (concrete blocks, brick) due to surface tension. This is why we see "efflorescence"‚Äîthat white, salty crust on walls.

Efflorescence vs. Mold:

1. Efflorescence dissolves in water; Mold does not.
2. Efflorescence is crystalline/salty; Mold is organic/filamentous.

XXX. Encapsulation Standards

When **we** write a protocol for a wet crawlspace, we often specify:

  • Vapor Barrier: Minimum 12-mil to 20-mil poly, seams taped and sealed to the piers.
  • Dehumidification: Sized for the square footage. Set to keep RH below 55%.
  • Foundation Vents: Sealed shut in a modern "closed" system.
Psychrometrics in the Pit:
Cool crawlspaces + Humid summer air = Condensation on floor joists.
*Watch for "hanging" insulation‚Äîit traps moisture against the wood!*

Sampling & Chain of Custody

If it wasn't documented correctly, it never happened.

XXXIII. Air Sampling Protocols

Precision is everything. We use calibrated pumps to ensure the Total Volume is accurate for the lab math.

Spore Trap Logistics:

  • Flow Rate: Typically 15 Liters Per Minute (LPM).
  • Duration: 5 to 10 minutes (Clean vs. Dusty environments).
  • Calibration: Always use a Rotameter or Primary Calibrator before and after sampling.

XXXIV. Surface Sampling (The Sticky Stuff)

  • Tape Lifts: Best for identifying the "Fungal Signature" of visible growth without destroying the surface.
  • Swabs: Used for irregular surfaces or when culturable (viable) samples are needed to determine species.
  • Bulk Samples: Taking a piece of the drywall/carpet to the lab.
Chain of Custody (COC):

The COC is a legal document that tracks the sample from the moment **we** take it until the lab disposes of it.

*Must include: Sample ID, Date/Time, Volume, Collector Name, and Relinquish Signature.*

XXXV. Shipping & Handling

Viable samples (agar plates) must be shipped overnight with cold packs. Non-viable spore traps are more durable but should be protected from extreme heat.

Lab Math: Spores counted / Volume = Spores per $m^3$

XXXVI. Key Definitions

Hyphal Fragments:

These are the "roots" or "branches" of the mold colony. Finding them in an air sample is a huge red flag. It indicates active growth nearby that is mechanically breaking apart and entering the air. Spores are the seeds; Hyphae are the plants.

Background Debris (Rating 1-5):

This measures how much "junk" (dust, skin cells, soot) is on the slide.
‚Ä¢ Rating 1-2: Clean slide; easy to count.
‚Ä¢ Rating 4-5: Overloaded. There is so much dust that spores are hidden behind it. This can lead to a False Negative.

XXXVII. Scenario Analysis

The Verdict: Even if the total count was low, the "Above Normal" Hyphal Fragments and the Asp/Pen dominance (12,400 vs 450) tell us this is a Condition 3 environment.

CIE Tip: High debris (Rating 4+) means you should re-sample with a shorter duration (e.g., 3 mins instead of 10) to get a clearer slide.

Gram-Negative Bacteria

The invisible chemical threat in water damage.

XXXVIII. Why "Gram-Negative"?

It refers to the Gram Stain test. These bacteria have a dual-layer cell wall that does not retain the violet stain. In our world, that wall is a toxic ticking time bomb.

The Endotoxin Factor (LPS):

Gram-negative bacteria contain Lipopolysaccharides (LPS) in their outer membrane.
‚Ä¢ When the bacteria die and the cell wall breaks apart, LPS is released as an Endotoxin.
‚Ä¢ Endotoxins are "pyrogens" (they cause fever/inflammation) and are not killed by standard biocides.

XXXIX. Primary Sources in Buildings

  • Sewage Backups: E. coli, Salmonella, and Pseudomonas.
  • HVAC Systems: Standing water in drain pans or "dirty" humidifiers.
  • Cooling Towers: The breeding ground for Legionella pneumophila.
Legionella Protocol:

If we suspect Legionella, we don't just take an air sample. We take bulk water samples from the source. Inhalation of mist (aerosolized water) is the primary exposure route.

XL. Testing for Bacteria

We use ATP Meters for a quick check, but for the exam, remember:

LAL Assay (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate):
The standard lab test used to quantify Endotoxin levels in dust or air.
*Note: Endotoxins can trigger asthma even if the bacteria are dead!*

Combustion & Soil Gases

The house is a breathing organism.

XLI. Combustion Backdrafting

When we put a house under negative pressure with air scrubbers or powerful exhaust fans, we can suck Carbon Monoxide (CO) back down the chimney.

The Worst-Case Scenario:

  • The Setup: Gas water heater + Tight house + Large exhaust fan.
  • The Result: Flue gases (CO) enter the living space instead of exhausting out the roof.
  • CIE Rule: Always test for spillage at the draft hood under "worst-case" depressurization.

XLII. Radon (The Silent Soil Gas)

Radon-222 is a radioactive gas from decaying Uranium in soil. It is the #2 cause of lung cancer.

The Numbers to Know:

1. Action Level: 4.0 pCi/L (Picocuries per liter).
2. Testing: Done in the "lowest livable area" (Basement/Crawl).
3. Mitigation: Sub-slab depressurization (venting the soil air out).

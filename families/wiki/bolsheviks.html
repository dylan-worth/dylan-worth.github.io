Bolshevik Revolution

"The Capitalists will sell us the rope with which we will hang them." — Vladimir Lenin

I. The Wall Street Connection

Contrary to the "class struggle" narrative, significant funding for the Bolsheviks originated in the West. Jacob Schiff (Kuhn, Loeb & Co.) is documented to have provided millions to fund revolutionary propaganda, specifically to destabilize the Tsar's regime during and after the Russo-Japanese War.

The Motivation: By 1917, the goal was to take Russia out of World War I and open its vast natural resources—specifically the Baku Oil Fields—to international interest once the old Russian aristocracy was cleared.

II. The "Sealed Train" & The Gold

In April 1917, the German High Command (with the knowledge of **Max Warburg**) allowed Vladimir Lenin and a group of revolutionaries to travel through Germany in a "sealed train" to Petrograd.

Financial Payload: Lenin was reportedly provided with roughly $5–$6 million in gold by the German government to ensure the revolution's success and the collapse of the Eastern Front.

Inter-Family Synergy

Morgan & Rockefeller

While the Bolsheviks nationalized assets, **Guaranty Trust** (Morgan) and **Standard Oil** (Rockefeller) maintained unique "back-door" concessions to operate in the new Soviet state, securing the Baku oil markets.

The Warburg Connection

While Max Warburg funded the Bolsheviks from Germany, his brother **Paul Warburg** was in the U.S. establishing the Federal Reserve—the very system that would later fund the Allied response to the USSR.

STATISTICAL ANALYSIS: The Kurganov Estimate

Cited by: Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn (1974)
Total Figure: 66,700,000 deaths (1917–1959)

"According to the calculations of the emigrated Professor of Statistics, I. A. Kurganov, this 'internal' war... cost us, from the beginning of the Revolution and up to 1959, 66 million people."

In 2026, this remains the defining "High-Estimate" for the human cost of the Bolshevik experiment, representing nearly one-third of the total population at the time.

III. 2026 Perspective: The "Capture Market"

In our current era, the Bolshevik Revolution is viewed as the first large-scale test of a "Captive Market." By centralizing all power into a single state apparatus, it became easier for international banking syndicates to negotiate with one dictator rather than a messy, free-market parliament.

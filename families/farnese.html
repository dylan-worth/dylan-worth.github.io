The House of Farnese

From Warlords to Princes of the Church

The Farnese family was a powerful Italian noble family originating in the Lazio region, near Rome. Their roots trace back to the 10th century, where they initially gained influence as feudal lords and military commanders (condottieri) serving the Papacy.

The Papal Peak: The family's destiny changed forever in 1534 with the election of Alessandro Farnese as Pope Paul III. His reign was a turning point for the family, as he practiced significant nepotism to establish the Farnese as sovereign dukes.

The Duchy of Parma and Piacenza

In 1545, Pope Paul III carved out the Duchies of Parma and Piacenza from the Papal States for his son, Pier Luigi Farnese. This established the family as a reigning European dynasty, a status they maintained until the mid-18th century.

Patrons of the Arts

The Farnese name is synonymous with the High Renaissance. They commissioned some of Rome's most magnificent structures, most notably the Palazzo Farnese (often called the most beautiful palace in Rome) and the Villa Farnese at Caprarola.

Their massive collection of ancient sculptures, known as the Farnese Collection, remains one of the most important in the world, including the famous Farnese Hercules and the Farnese Bull.

The Fleur-de-lis

The Farnese coat of arms is instantly recognizable for its six blue fleurs-de-lis on a golden background. This symbol remains a prominent architectural feature throughout Rome and Parma today.

Four Years in the Field: Lessons from iMold

As I’m creeping up on four years at iMold, I’ll say that throughout my time here, each year definitely carried a different title.

The Year of Confusion

My first year was the year of confusion. I learned the art of six-side cleaning. It wasn’t your typical clean where you dust your shelves, wipe down counters, or mop floors. It was a tedious process of cleaning every square inch of the house. This included wiping down every item inside a home — including the insides of cabinets. I used toothbrushes and Microban on window tracks, sliding doors, and even lock mechanisms. I wiped down every Lego, rinsed, drained, and dried aquarium rocks, and even cleaned seashells — because spores are microscopic and can land anywhere.

The Year of Demolition

My second year was the year I focused heavily on demolition. I switched crew leads and became more independent, as my new lead had more of a partner dynamic rather than a boss-and-helper structure. We figured things out through trial and error and learned the more efficient ways to take things apart and put them back together. We busted our butts restoring homes damaged by Hurricane Ian.

The Year of Discovery

My third year was the year of discovery. I started understanding the science behind mold. I learned that something as small as a baseboard separating could either be worn caulk — or a sign of water damage. I tore out plenty of walls only to find heavy microbial growth behind them. I started learning how air handlers worked and mastered the process of cleaning blowers. I gained confidence and stepped into a lead role.

The Year of Direction

My fourth year became the year of direction. I found myself writing scopes in my head just based on visual observations. I started leading more, sharing knowledge with my team, and realized I don’t want to remediate forever — I want to prevent the damage before it happens. That led me down the path toward becoming a mold assessor. Spores are invisible to the human eye, so visual inspections alone won’t give you the full story. You also won’t know what type of microbial growth is present. If it’s Aspergillus, I might suggest cleaning the carpet. But if it’s Stachybotrys? I’d recommend disposing of it.

This post is just a reflection. Lately, I’ve been thinking a lot about this company, the growth, and how far I’ve come.
